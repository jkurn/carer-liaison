# Design: Carer Liaison — Finding the Emotional Core

Generated by /office-hours on 2026-03-27
Branch: main
Status: APPROVED
Mode: Startup

## Problem Statement

The Carer Liaison landing page headline has been rewritten 5+ times in a single session and still doesn't land. The founder (Jonathan) is building from deep lived experience — his mum is the primary carer for his brother (28 years, non-verbal, autistic, global developmental delay, motoric disability). Every headline written from frameworks, competitor research, and positioning theory has failed because it describes the problem from the outside rather than from the inside.

## Demand Evidence

This is a founder-as-user product. The demand evidence is Jonathan's family:

- **Mum (60):** Primary carer for 28 years. Tracks nutrition, medication, bowel movements, naps, sleep, chewing constraints in a combination of notebooks, WhatsApp screenshots with annotated clocks, and her head. Has migraines and muscle issues from the physical and emotional toll. Constantly alert, which causes her to lash out at family. She IS the system — when she has a bad day, everything falls apart.
- **Dad (61):** Helps when he can but runs a business. Limited daily availability.
- **Brother (28):** Non-verbal, autistic, global developmental delay, motoric disability. Not toilet trained. Communicates through facilitated communication only with mum. Needs stimulation and purpose — feels great when giving advice through FC, but it's physically exhausting for mum.
- **Family system:** Currently split between Indonesia (affordable domestic help) and Australia (better disability care). Want to get brother on NDIS but daily living costs are prohibitive. At their wits' end.

The demand is not hypothetical. This family needs this product.

## Status Quo

Mum's current "system":
- Notebook for food tracking
- WhatsApp screenshots with clock annotations for things she can't forget
- Phone screenshots annotated with timestamps
- 28 years of pattern recognition stored entirely in her head
- Verbal requests to family members ("message me on WhatsApp")
- Fitness tracker for brother, checked occasionally
- No service discovery system — doesn't know what's available or affordable
- No NDIS plan yet — blocked by cost of daily living in Australia

This system works on good days. On bad days (poor sleep, migraine, emotional exhaustion), it falls apart — because mum IS the system.

## Target User & Narrowest Wedge

**Target user:** Mum. Specifically: unpaid family carers (parents, partners, siblings) of people with complex disability who have been caring for years, have deep accumulated knowledge about their person, and have no system to hold that knowledge outside their own head.

**Narrowest wedge:** The AI carer journal. It's the right entry point because:
1. Zero setup — just talk about your day
2. Validates the carer (not just the care recipient)
3. Captures the knowledge that currently lives only in mum's head
4. Generates data that powers everything else later (health tracking, pattern recognition, service discovery)
5. The demo already exists

## Constraints

- Pre-product / pre-NDIS — can't promise NDIS funding alignment yet
- Target users (60-year-old carers) may not be tech-confident
- Emotional sensitivity — disability caring is not a "problem to solve," it's a life
- The product must feel like a companion, not a tool
- Must work for someone who is exhausted, on edge, and has no spare attention

## Premises

1. **The headline failed because it described the problem from the outside (systems, NDIS, invisible work) instead of from the inside (what the morning actually feels like).** — AGREED
2. **The AI carer journal is the right wedge** — lowest friction, validates the carer, generates the data for everything else. — AGREED
3. **The emotional core is not burden, not invisibility, not loneliness. It's "it takes a village" — the need to feel like someone is alongside you in this journey.** — AGREED
4. **The good day feeling is "mise en place" — everything in its right place so the day flows instead of fights. That's what the product actually delivers.** — AGREED

## Approaches Considered

### Approach A: "Mum's Morning" — Write from specific lived moments
Take the raw details (screenshots, notebook, 28 years, chewing constraints) and build the headline from one specific moment. Rejected because it was too literal and too specific to one family — reads like a case study, not a landing page.

### Approach B: "The Words She Already Uses"
Get the headline from mum's actual language — how she describes her experience to others. Deferred as a future exercise (requires a conversation), but remains the gold standard for authentic copy.

### Approach C: "The Feeling, Not the Facts"
Stop describing what carers DO. Start with what it FEELS like. The feeling uncovered through this session: **"it takes a village" — I need someone alongside me in this journey.** Combined with: **the good day is mise en place — everything in its right place so the day flows.**

## Recommended Approach

**Approach C** — but informed by the specificity of Approach A.

The headline should make a carer feel: "someone is here with me." The subhead should make them feel: "and they understand what a good day looks like."

The emotional equation:
```
HEADLINE  = "You're not alone in this" (village feeling)
SUBHEAD   = "Everything in its right place" (mise en place)
PRODUCT   = "The companion that helps the day flow"
```

This is NOT a positioning framework. This is the emotional truth of one family, generalized just enough to resonate with every carer.

### Candidate directions (not final copy — directions to write from):

**Direction 1: The village**
- Headline territory: "Caring was never meant to be done alone"
- Risk: could sound like a charity tagline

**Direction 2: The companion**
- Headline territory: "A system that walks alongside you"
- Risk: "system" is cold next to "alongside"

**Direction 3: The mise en place**
- Headline territory: "So the day flows instead of fights"
- Risk: might not land for people who don't know the good-day feeling yet

**Direction 4: The blend**
- Headline territory: Combine village + mise en place — you're not alone, and together we make the day flow
- This is the most promising but needs craft

## Open Questions

1. **Has mum used the AI journal demo?** This is the single highest-leverage action. Watching her use it will generate the headline, the copy, and the product roadmap in one session.
2. **What does mum actually say when she describes a hard day to a friend?** Those words are the headline.
3. **Does the "village" feeling resonate with other carers, or is it specific to this family's situation?** Needs validation with 2-3 other carers.
4. **Is the journal the product, or is it the wedge into the operating system?** This affects how the landing page frames the long-term vision.
5. **Indonesia vs Australia tension** — if the family is currently in Indonesia, how does the product serve them NOW vs when they return to Australia for NDIS?

## Success Criteria

- Jonathan's mum reads the headline and has a "hell yes body moment"
- Jonathan's sister, dad, and brother also feel it
- A carer in an NDIS Facebook group would stop scrolling
- The headline could NOT have been written by any other product in the competitor landscape
- The headline leads naturally to the AI journal as the entry point

## Dependencies

- A conversation with mum (watching her use the journal, hearing her language)
- Validation with 2-3 carers outside the family
- Decision on whether to launch with the journal wedge or the full operating system vision

## The Assignment

**Do not rewrite the headline again.**

Instead, do this:

1. **This week:** Sit next to your mum. Open the AI journal demo. Say "just talk about your day." Watch. Don't help. Write down:
   - The exact words she uses
   - Where she hesitates
   - What she says after the reflection card appears
   - Whether she wants to do it again tomorrow

2. **Listen for the headline.** It will come from her mouth, not from a framework. It might be something she says about the journal ("oh, it's like having someone who..."). It might be something she says about her day that the journal captures. It might be the look on her face.

3. **Ask her one question after:** "What would you call this, if you were telling your friend about it?"

The answer to that question is your headline.

## What I noticed about how you think

- You said "it takes a village" — that's not a product insight, that's a life insight. The best products are built from those. You're not optimizing a funnel, you're trying to give your family something real.

- You rejected 5+ headline options not because they were bad copy, but because they didn't pass the "mum test." That's taste. Most founders would have shipped the second draft. You're holding out for truth, and that's why this product has a chance to be genuinely different.

- You said the good day feeling is "mise en place — everything going according to her spec in the right order so life is effortless." That's the product vision in one sentence. Not an operating system. Not a tracker. Mise en place for caring. If you build that, you build something no one else has.

- You described six distinct problems and then immediately tried to turn them into one platform. That's the founder instinct to build the cathedral. The discipline is to build the kitchen first — and the AI journal is the kitchen.
